Understanding the ASIC Design Flow

By Abdelhameed Mahmoud | Mar 8, 2022

Creating an Application-Specific Integrated Circuit (ASIC) is a complex but rewarding process. This article breaks down the standard ASIC design flow, from initial specification to final tapeout.

We will explore key stages including RTL (Register Transfer Level) design, functional verification, logic synthesis, place and route, and physical verification. Understanding this flow is crucial for any engineer looking to design custom silicon. The journey from a high-level idea to a physical chip involves a series of highly specialized steps, each with its own set of challenges and tools.

The "RTL-to-GDSII" Flow

The ASIC design process is often called the "RTL-to-GDSII" flow, which summarizes the journey from abstract code to a physical layout file.

  1. RTL Design: Describing the chip's functionality in Verilog or VHDL.
  2. Functional Verification: Simulating the RTL to ensure it behaves as expected.
  3. Logic Synthesis: Converting the RTL into a gate-level netlist using a standard cell library.
  4. Floorplanning: Planning the high-level layout of the chip.
  5. Placement & Routing: Placing the standard cells and routing the connections between them.
  6. Physical Verification: Checking that the final layout adheres to all manufacturing rules (DRC/LVS).
  7. Tapeout: Sending the final GDSII file to the foundry for manufacturing.
